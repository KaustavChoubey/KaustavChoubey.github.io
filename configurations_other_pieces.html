CONFIGURATIONS INVOLVING OTHER PIECES

The aim of this subsection is to create a program which analyses whether the configurations involving other pieces like Bishops, Kings, Queens and Knights are in a challenging configuration and later through millions of simulations of random placings of those pieces on the board, we are going to calculate the probability that 2 similar chess pieces placed randomly will challenge each other. The result from the simulation will then be verified through mathematical modelling of the situation.

2 BISHOPS ON THE BOARD

Consider 2 bishops: Bishop 1 and Bishop 2 which are going to be placed randomly on distinct boxes of the chessboard. Two bishops challenge each other only when they lie on the same diagonal. Suppose Bishop 1 is at C5. Then, Bishop 1 will attack only when another piece is at any diagonal box from A7 to G1 or at any diagonal box from A3 to F8. Now let $A\equiv1$, $B\equiv2$, $C\equiv3$, $D\equiv4$ and so till $H\equiv8$. A Bishop attacks another when either the sum of the row number and column number of the bishop is equal to the sum of those of the other piece or when the difference of the row number and column number of the bishop is equal to the difference of those of that piece. For example, by the above fashion $C+5\equiv8$ and $A+7\equiv8.$ Hence, the bishop at C5 attacks the piece at A7. The below program uses this logic to find if 2 bishops are in challenging configuration.

random.seed()

n=2
z_coordinate=[0]*n
y_coordinate=[0]*n
x_coordinate=[0]*n

title="\u265d"

#Program to assign distinct positions to all the kings
for i in range(n):
  y_coordinate[i]=random.randrange(1,9)
  z_coordinate[i]=random.randrange(65,73)-64
  x_coordinate[i]=chr(z_coordinate[i]+64)

  for j in range(i): 
    while z_coordinate[i]==z_coordinate[j] and y_coordinate[i]==y_coordinate[j]:

      z_coordinate[i]=random.randrange(65,73)-64

case1=z_coordinate[0]-y_coordinate[0]==z_coordinate[1]-y_coordinate[1]
case2=z_coordinate[0]+y_coordinate[0]==z_coordinate[1]+y_coordinate[1]

xlabels=["A","B","C","D","E","F","G","H"]
plt.xticks(range(1,9),xlabels)

#Draws the checkered board
x=np.linspace(0.5,1.5,40)
for j in range(8):
  for i in range(j%2,8,2):
    for k in x:
      plt.plot([k+i,i+0.5],[j+0.5,j+k],color="grey")
      if k!=0.5:
        plt.plot([k+i,i+1.5],[j+1.5,k+j],color="grey")

for i in range(n):
  if case1 or case2:
    for k in x:
      plt.plot([k+z_coordinate[i]-1,z_coordinate[i]-0.5],[y_coordinate[i]-0.5,y_coordinate[i]+k-1],color="red")
      if k!=0.5:
        plt.plot([k+z_coordinate[i]-1,z_coordinate[i]+0.5],[y_coordinate[i]+0.5,k+y_coordinate[i]-1],color="red")
  else:
    for k in x:
      plt.plot([k+z_coordinate[i]-1,z_coordinate[i]-0.5],[y_coordinate[i]-0.5,y_coordinate[i]+k-1],color="lawngreen")
      if k!=0.5:
        plt.plot([k+z_coordinate[i]-1,z_coordinate[i]+0.5],[y_coordinate[i]+0.5,k+y_coordinate[i]-1],color="lawngreen")

for i in range(n):
  plt.text((z_coordinate[i]-1)+0.65, (y_coordinate[i]-1)+0.7, title,fontsize=26)

Mathematical Investigation: The probability that 2 Bishops land in non challenging configuration is 0.86.

The above chessboard has been divided into 4 zones: Zone A, Zone B, Zone C and Zone D. In Zone A, a bishop has a total attacking range of 11 boxes. In Zone B, the bishop has a total attacking range of 9 boxes. In Zone C, the bishop has a total attacking range of 7 boxes and in Zone D, the bishop has a total attacking range of 13 boxes. We are going to consider the following 4 disjoint cases:

  • Case A: When Bishop 1 is in Zone A and Bishop 2 anywhere on the board such that they don't challenge each other.

  • Case B: When Bishop 1 is in Zone B and Bishop 2 anywhere on the board such that they don't challenge each other.

  • Case C: When Bishop 1 is in Zone C and Bishop 2 anywhere on the board such that they don't challenge each other.

  • Case D: When Bishop 1 is in Zone D and Bishop 2 anywhere on the board such that they don't challenge each other.

Now, the probability that the 2 Bishops land in non-challenging configuration is $P(\text{Case A}\cup \text{Case B}\cup\text {Case C}\cup \text{Case D})$. Now as Case A, Case B, Case C and Case D are 4 disjoint events so $P(\text{Case A}\cup \text{Case B}\cup \text{Case C}\cup \text{Case D})=P(\text{Case A})+P(\text{Case B})+P(\text{Case C})+P(\text{Case D}).$ So let us first find out the number of ways in which Case A can happen.

If Bishop 1 lands anywhere in Zone A then it can land on any of the 12 boxes. As it has now has a target range of 11 boxes so there remains $63-11=52$ boxes where Bishop 2 can land so that they don't challenge each other. Hence, there are a total of $52\times 12$ ways in which Case A can happen.

Now let us consider Case B. If Bishop 1 lands anywhere in Zone B then it can land on any of the 20 boxes. As it has now has a target range of 9 boxes so there remains $63-9=54$ boxes where Bishop 2 can land so that they don't challenge each other. Hence, there are a total of $54\times20$ ways in which Case B can happen.

Consider Case C. If Bishop 1 lands anywhere in Zone C then it can land on any of the 28 boxes. At any box of Zone C, Bishop 1 has a target range of 7 boxes so there are a total of $28\times56$ ways in which Case C can happen.

Similarly, there are a total of $4\times50$ ways in which Case D can happen as in Zone D a Bishop has a target range of 13 boxes and there are 4 boxes in Zone D.

Now there are a total of $64\times63$ ways of placing the 2 Bishops on the board. So: \begin{equation*} P(Case A)=\frac{52\times12}{64\times63}=0.1548. \end{equation*} \begin{equation*} P(Case B)=\frac{54\times20}{64\times63}=0.2678 \end{equation*} \begin{equation*} P(Case C)=\frac{28\times56}{64\times63}=0.3888 \end{equation*} \begin{equation*} P(Case D)=\frac{4\times50}{64\times63}=0.0496 \end{equation*}

So, $P(\text{Case A }\cup \text{ Case B }\cup\text { Case C}\cup\text{ Case D})=P(\text{Case A})+P(\text{Case B})+P(\text{Case C})+P(\text{Case D})$ $=0.1548+0.2678+0.3888+0.0496=0.861\approx0.86$.
